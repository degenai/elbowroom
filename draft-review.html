What Cupping Actually Does (and the Marks)

Drafted
modality explainer carousel educational
HookThose round marks aren't bruises. Here's what cupping is really doing.

Modality explainer for cupping: high curiosity, high save potential, and it preempts the "what are those circles?" question. Plain-language mechanism, honest about the marks (they're not bruises in the injury sense), no overclaims. Offered as one tool among many, dialed to the person.

Caption draft

Cupping uses gentle suction to lift the tissue instead of pressing down into it, basically the opposite direction of most massage. That decompression can give tight, stuck areas a little room to move and draws blood flow to the spot.

About the marks: they show up when there's visible mark in an area, and they fade over a few days. They're not the painful kind of bruise, and darker doesn't mean "better". It's just information about what that tissue was holding.

It's one tool, not the whole toolbox. Whether cupping makes sense for you depends on what your body's asking for that day, which is the point of building each session around you.

Curious if it'd help? Save this and ask at your next visit.

What Is a "Knot," Really?

Drafted
modality explainer carousel question
HookYou call it a knot. Here's what your muscle is actually doing.

Modality explainer for trigger point work, anchored to the word everyone already uses: "knot." Explains what a trigger point is and how the work addresses it (sustained pressure, pin-and-stretch), and why it can refer pain elsewhere. Pairs naturally with er-005 (referred pain). Education, not an overpromise.

Caption draft

A "knot" usually isn't a tangle. It's a small band of muscle fibers that got stuck in a contracted state and won't let go. We call those trigger points.

Two things make them sneaky: → They can refer pain somewhere else, so the sore spot isn't always the knot. → They tend to come back if the reason they formed (posture, overuse, an old compensation) never changed.

Trigger point work is basically convincing that band to release with steady, specific pressure, often followed by stretching the muscle back to its full length so it doesn't just re-grip.

It's not a magic-button fix, it's a conversation with the tissue. Save this for the next time someone says "I've just got a knot."

Pin-and-Stretch: Why We Move You

Drafted
modality explainer carousel educational
HookWait, am I supposed to move during a massage? Sometimes, yes.

Modality explainer for the active techniques, pin-and-stretch and PNF, that surprise people because they involve movement, not just lying still. Explains why combining pressure with motion can get more length out of a muscle than either alone. Sets the expectation that a clinical session may ask you to move, breathe, or resist. No overclaims.

Caption draft

Not every minute of a good massage is you lying perfectly still. Some of the most effective work is active.

Pin-and-stretch is exactly what it sounds like: we anchor a spot on a tight muscle, then take the limb through a stretch so the tissue lengthens *under* that pressure. You get more release than pressing or stretching alone would give.

PNF is the cousin: you gently push against us, then relax, and the muscle lets go a little further each round. Your nervous system does half the work.

So if your therapist asks you to take a breath, push, or let a leg move, that's not them improvising. That's the technique. Save this so it makes sense when it happens.
